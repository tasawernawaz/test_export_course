Bottle Filling Process in BathTime BathTime is a Cambridge-based company that makes a full range of liquid products, including bath gels, shampoos, lotions, and creams. The production of such products involves three major steps: mixing, filling and packing.

  • Mixing: Chemicals are put together according to formulas, mixed in bulk, and stored in tanks.
  • Filling: Bottles printed with product specifications are lined up, filled with liquid product, and capped with lids.
  • Packing: Bottles are organized in sets of a dozen in plastic shrink-wrap and packed into cardboard boxes as finished goods.

As BathTime competes on the quality of its products, inspections are performed at the end of each process. Consider the filling of BathTime’s leading product: Top-to-Toe Bath 1000 ml. A portion of the production process flow is illustrated in Figure 1.

The bottle arrival process at the filling machine is Poisson with rate of 60 per minute. The filling time (including inspection) is exponential with a mean of 0.5 second. The purpose of inspection after filling is to check the volume of product. Bottles with volume under 990 ml need refilling at the filling machine. The total volume of bottle is 1050 ml. Overfilling will make the outside of the bottle sticky and it will be scrapped. The bottles that pass inspection are considered qualified and go to the capping machine. According to statistics from BathTime’s process engineers2, the filling volume follows a symmetric triangular distribution between 980 ml and 1060 ml with a mean of 1020 ml. The capping machine can complete 5400 bottles per hour, on the average. According to BathTime’s process engineers, the time to cap each bottle is exponentially distributed. The inspection just after capping process has four possible outcomes for each bottle:

  • Missing cap, with probability at 0.1. The bottle needs to be recapped at the capping machine.
  • Spilled liquid, with probability 0.06. The bottle needs to be refilled at the filling machine and then go through capping process again.
  • Misalignment between cap and bottle which damages the edge of the bottle, with probability 0.04. The bottle is scrapped.
  • Success. The bottle is qualified and it leaves the system.

Several assumptions are made here, including:

  • Both the filling machine and the capping machine are perfectly reliable, in the sense that they do not fail in a mode that requires repair. To put it another way, they are always available.
  • All queues in the system are without capacity constraints, i.e., there is no upper limit to the length of any queue.
  • In filling and capping, the rework flow is treated identically to the original arrival flow.
